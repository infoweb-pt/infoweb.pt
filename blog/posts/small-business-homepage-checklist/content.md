# What Should Be on a Small Business Homepage?

Your homepage is not a brochure. It is the first sales conversation most visitors have with your business. If they land on a vague page, they leave. If they land on a clear page, they call, book, or request a quote.

This checklist covers the homepage anatomy that actually converts for local shops, clinics, trades, restaurants, and service businesses.

## The job of a homepage

A good homepage answers four questions in under 10 seconds:

1. **What do you do?**
2. **Who is it for?**
3. **Why should I trust you?**
4. **What should I do next?**

Everything else is supporting detail.

## Homepage checklist (in order)

### 1. Clear promise above the fold

The headline should describe the outcome, not your company history.

**Vague:** “Welcome to Smith & Co”

**Useful:** “Emergency plumbing in Manchester — same-day call-outs”

Pair the headline with one supporting line and one primary button (call, book, or get a quote).

### 2. Services or offers people search for

Do not hide services inside a generic “About” page. List the top 3–6 services with plain language:

- Boiler repair
- Bathroom installs
- Drain unblocking

Each item can link to a dedicated service page later. That helps SEO and clarity.

### 3. Proof that reduces risk

Pick at least two proof types:

- Google review score with a link to reviews
- Short client quotes with name and business type
- Years in business or certifications
- Before/after photos for trades and beauty

Proof beats adjectives. “Trusted provider” means less than “4.9★ from 120+ Google reviews.”

### 4. Opening hours and response time

For local businesses, hours are conversion content. Add:

- Today’s hours or weekly schedule
- Holiday exceptions when relevant
- “We reply within 2 hours on weekdays” if that is true

### 5. Contact options that match how customers act

Offer more than one path:

- Click-to-call phone number on mobile
- WhatsApp or contact form
- Online booking link if you take appointments

Put the phone number in the header on mobile. Many visitors are ready to act immediately.

### 6. Location and service area

Add a map embed or address block. For service businesses, state the towns or postcodes you cover. This helps local SEO and stops unqualified enquiries.

### 7. Short FAQ (3–5 questions)

Answer the objections that block contact:

- “Do you charge call-out fees?”
- “Are you insured?”
- “How fast can you come?”

FAQs also give Google more relevant text on the page.

### 8. One primary CTA repeated wisely

Choose one main action: **Call**, **Book**, or **Get a quote**. Repeat it in the hero, after services, and at the bottom. Secondary links (Instagram, email) should not compete with the main action.

## Before/after copy examples

### Restaurant

**Before:** “Fine dining experience in the city centre.”

**After:** “Italian lunch & dinner in Bristol city centre — book a table or order collection.”

### Clinic

**Before:** “Committed to patient wellness.”

**After:** “Physiotherapy in Leeds — new patients welcome, online booking available.”

### Trades

**Before:** “Quality workmanship guaranteed.”

**After:** “Electrician in Dublin — fault finding, rewires, landlord certificates — free quotes.”

## Common homepage mistakes

- Stock photo of a handshake instead of real work
- Slider with five messages and zero clarity
- “Learn more” buttons with no specific next step
- No phone link on mobile
- Services written for insiders, not customers

## Quick self-audit

Open your homepage on your phone and ask:

- Can I understand the offer in 5 seconds?
- Is the main button obvious?
- Can I call or book in one tap?
- Do I see proof and location without scrolling forever?

If any answer is no, fix the homepage before spending on ads or SEO.

## Bottom line

A small business homepage should make the next step obvious. Clarity beats clever design. When the promise, services, proof, hours, contact paths, and CTA are in place, the rest of the site supports the sale instead of replacing it.
